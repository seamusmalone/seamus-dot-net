Teaching Philosophy

I teach art with a focus on the production of meaning, both in the creation and the interpretation of work. I teach with an emphasis on the development of extralinguistic communication skills, critical thinking (phenomenology, perception, viewer, embodiment) I think these skills are the most fundamental for those seeking a career as a professional artist, but are ultimately no less valuable for students seeking to use the arts as a secondary skill in their career, as an outlet for self exploration or greater cultural understanding.

Interpretive critiques are central to the development of these abilities. In preparation for critiques students are asked to write a one page artists statement outlining the ideas that they would like to communicate to an audience. Once the work has been presented, critiques begin with the audiences reaction to the work. These reactions are neither qualitative appraisals of the works aesthetic merits nor () emotional reactions, but audience contributions toward answering the question "what do you think the artist is saying". While I don't overtly discourage emotional or aesthetic reaction, I attempt to prompt students to see how these reactions contextualize and contribute to the meaning of the work, rather than constitute a () reaction in themselves.

Once this part of the critique is finished and all opinions have been expressed and several possibilities explored, the artist is asked to read their artists statement. Finally we look at the discrepancies between the audiences understanding of the work and the artists intentions. We address both the successes and failures, if the audience "got it" why? how? If they did not, what could the artist have to done to address the misunderstandings, how could they have provided more context or clues within the work that would have helped the audience come closer to understanding his or her aims? It would be a misunderstanding to take all this talk about sign systems to de-emphasize the practice of making art. The ability to create meaning is ultimately in the material practices we engage. In fact denotative language within a piece is discouraged, and generally shown to be crutch, made necessary by a failure to understand that the same information could be communicated in the "native media" of the work.

Through this means, students not only develop an understanding of their own art, but become more savvy cultural consumers, capable of decoding the ideological subtext of the non-verbal systems in which they are immersed, be it art, film, architecture and television or more broadly those 'unthought' non-verbal sign systems in which we all are embedded, professional, public-political and interpersonal relationships.
